§ The brief

One question, asked of five million rides.

Cyclistic's marketing director believes future growth depends on converting casual riders into annual members, who are significantly more profitable.

To design a targeted conversion campaign, the team needs a clear, evidence-backed answer to a single question — one with margin-shifting consequences if we get it right.

“How do annual members and casual riders use Cyclistic bikes differently?”

Three findings. Three recommendations. Reproducible R pipeline. The rest of this page walks through what the data said.

01 Ride length

Casual rides are roughly twice as long.

Members average around 12 minutes per ride. Casuals average 22+ minutes. The gap holds every day of the week — and widens on weekends.

This is the single most important behavioral signal in the dataset. Members use bikes as a transportation tool. Casuals use them as a leisure activity. Same product, two different jobs.

02 Hour of day

Members spike at rush hour. Casuals don’t.

Plot rides by hour and the use-case difference becomes unmistakable. Members post a textbook two-peak commute curve at 8 AM and 5 PM.

Casuals show a single broad afternoon hump centered on 2–4 PM — a leisure shape, not a transportation shape. Two segments. Two products. One dataset.

03 Day of week

Weekdays belong to members. Weekends flip.

Member volume dominates Monday through Friday. Casual ride volume jumps Saturday and Sunday — and frequently exceeds member volume on those days.

Translation: the fastest path to a casual rider runs through their weekend, not their inbox on a Tuesday morning.

04 Seasonality

Both segments peak in summer. Casuals swing harder.

Both groups bottom out in January and crest in July. The casual curve, though, is much steeper — a roughly 6–8× winter-to-summer swing, versus 3–4× for members.

That means the conversion window is real and bounded: marketing spend should be front-loaded into spring, not spread evenly across the year.

05 Geography

Casual rides cluster at the lakefront.

The top casual start stations are dominated by Streeter Drive, DuSable Lake Shore stations, Millennium Park, and Navy Pier — all leisure and tourist locations.

Member rides, by contrast, are far more evenly distributed across the network. The geography itself tells you where to spend the marketing dollar.

∑ The summary

Same product. Different jobs.

Members and casuals are not slightly different versions of the same customer. They are using the same bikes for fundamentally different jobs. Any conversion strategy that misses that is structurally underpriced.

→ Recommendations

Three plays the marketing team can run this quarter.

1

Launch a “Weekend & Leisure” membership tier

A standard annual membership is built for daily commuters — casuals don’t see themselves in that product. Price a Fri–Sun unlimited tier (Mar–Oct, 60-min rides) to beat 2–3 day-pass purchases per month. Sell against a need they’re already demonstrating.

Pricing · Product
2

Geo-target the top 15 casual stations

The lakefront stations are physical, repeatable, high-traffic touchpoints. Push in-app conversion offers when a casual docks there; deploy QR-code signage at the docks themselves; concentrate paid social geo-fences on those station footprints, May → September.

Growth · Channel mix
3

Front-load the conversion push, May → July

Casual ridership roughly triples between April and July. A May convert captures three more months of value than an August one. Front-load the budget into spring; lead with cost-per-ride math, not commuting benefits.

Timing · Budget
⚙ The pipeline

Reproducible. One command.

The full analysis runs end-to-end in R via source("R/run_all.R"). ~5M rows, ~10 minutes, ~3 GB disk. No notebooks, no manual steps, no hidden state. Anyone with R ≥ 4.2 and an internet connection can rebuild every chart on this page from raw data.

R 4.2+ tidyverse lubridate ggplot2 janitor scales here

Cleaning rules: drop rows with missing timestamps, drop rides under 1 minute (false starts) or over 24 hours (lost bikes), enforce membership-type sanity. Roughly 1–2% of records are filtered, matching loss rates in published Divvy analyses.
